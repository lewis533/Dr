What Is Engineering Drawing?

The language of engineers — start here, understand this first

Engineering drawing is a way of communicating the exact shape, size, and layout of objects or buildings using lines and symbols — so precisely that someone on the other side of the world can build it without ever seeing the original. It's a universal language that every engineer must understand.

🎯 The Purpose

To describe a 3D object or building on flat paper in a way that is exact and unambiguous. Every line means something specific. Nothing is drawn randomly.

📐 Two Main Types You Need

  • Mechanical drawing — showing 3D machine parts from multiple directions (Assignment 1)
  • Architectural/electrical drawing — showing buildings and circuits from above (Assignment 2)

⚡ Why You Need This

As an electrical & telecom engineer, you'll draw circuit diagrams, building wiring layouts, equipment installations, and technical schematics throughout your entire career.

💡
Think of it like this: If you want to describe a room to a builder in another city, you can't bring the room. So you draw it from above (floor plan), mark every wall, window, door, socket, and light — with exact measurements. That's engineering drawing.

Key Concepts You Must Know

📏 Scale

Because you can't draw a house at actual size on paper, you scale it down. 1:50 means 1mm on paper = 50mm in real life. Always write the scale in your title block.

📦 Views

A "view" is what you see when you look at an object from one direction — flat, no depth, no perspective. Like a photograph taken perfectly straight on.

🔢 Dimensions

Numbers written on the drawing showing exact sizes (width, height, length, diameter). Without these, a drawing can't be built from.

🏷️ Title Block

A box in the bottom-right corner of every drawing. Contains: your name, registration number, drawing title, scale, date, and course.

Orthographic Projection — Turning 3D Into 2D

The core skill of mechanical engineering drawing

"Orthographic" means "at right angles." You place an object inside an imaginary glass box and look at it perfectly straight from each direction. Each face of the box becomes a 2D view. Then you unfold the box flat — and that layout IS your drawing.

🔑 The Golden Rules of Projection

  • All views must align. A hole in the front view must appear directly above it in the top view (same X position) and directly beside it in the side view (same Y position).
  • No perspective. Lines do not converge. Everything is flat and true to scale.
  • What you can see = solid line. What you can't see but exists = dashed line.
  • Each view shows exactly 2 dimensions. Front view shows width & height. Top view shows width & depth. Side view shows depth & height.

First Angle vs Third Angle Projection

Your assignment requires Third Angle — here's exactly what that means

🇺🇸 Third Angle Projection

Used in: USA, Canada, Kenya, Australia

The object is between the viewer and the paper. Views land on the same side you're looking from.

  • Top view → goes above front view
  • Left view → goes left of front view
  • Right view → goes right of front view

🇬🇧 First Angle Projection

Used in: Europe, UK, much of Africa

The paper is behind the object. Views land on the opposite side from where you look.

  • Top view → goes below front view
  • Left view → goes right of front view
  • Right view → goes left of front view
⚠️
How to tell which one a drawing uses: Look for the projection symbol — it's a small truncated cone drawn from two directions. Third angle symbol shows the wide end facing toward you. First angle shows the narrow end. Your assignment says "third angle projection" so use the left layout above.

How to Draw Floor Plans

What a floor plan is, how it works, and how to draw one in AutoCAD

A floor plan is a view of a building from directly above — as if you removed the roof and looked straight down. You see all the walls, rooms, doors, windows, and fittings. For Assignment 2, you also add electrical symbols and wiring on top of the floor plan.

🏠 What a Floor Plan Shows

  • Wall thickness and positions
  • Room names and sizes
  • Door openings (with swing arc)
  • Window positions (gaps in walls)
  • Fixed fittings (bath, toilet, sink)
  • Room dimensions

⚡ What Your Assignment Adds

  • Light positions (ceiling circles)
  • Socket positions (on walls)
  • Switch positions (on wall near doors)
  • Smoke detectors (R symbols)
  • Wiring routes (dashed coloured lines)
  • Consumer unit (main board)

How to Draw Walls in AutoCAD

1

Draw the outer wall outline

Switch to Objects layer. Use REC to draw the overall outer boundary of the house (e.g. @1200,1000 for a 12m × 10m house at 1:10 scale).

2

Create inner wall boundary (wall thickness)

Use O (Offset) to offset the outer rectangle inward by the wall thickness (e.g. 150mm for a standard brick wall). This creates the inner face of your outer walls.

O ← Offset command
→ Type wall thickness: 150
→ Click the outer rectangle
→ Click inside (toward centre) to offset inward
3

Draw internal partition walls

Use L (Line) or ML (Multiline) to draw the walls separating rooms. Standard internal walls are about 100mm thick. Snap to the inner wall face when placing.

4

Add door openings

Trim the wall line where the door goes using TR. Then draw the door: a straight line for the door leaf, and an arc showing the swing path.

TR ← Trim the wall gap for door
L ← Draw door leaf line
ARC ← Draw the swing arc
Start: hinge point, End: door tip, Center: hinge
5

Add window openings

Trim the wall where the window is. Draw two parallel lines inside the gap to represent the window frame. Windows don't have swing arcs.

6

Add text (room labels)

Switch to Dimensions layer. Use MTEXT to label each room: "Living Room", "Kitchen", "Bedroom", "WC" etc. Place labels in the centre of each room.

7

Add electrical symbols

Switch to a dedicated Electrical layer. Place ceiling lights (circles with cross), sockets (rectangles with circles), switches ($ symbol) in appropriate positions matching the reference diagram.

8

Draw wiring routes

Use curved lines (SPLINE or ARC) in different colors to connect switches to their lights. Each circuit should be a different color. Use dashed linetype on the wiring layer.

📐
Scale tip for Assignment 2: A typical house might be 15m × 12m. On an A3 sheet (420×297mm), drawing at 1:50 would make it 300×240mm — perfect. So in AutoCAD, draw at real size (e.g. 15000mm × 12000mm) and set the plot scale to 1:50 when printing. Always note the scale in your title block.

The 6 Layers — Setup in AutoCAD

Your assignment requires exactly 6 layers — here's what each does and how to create them

Layers are like transparent sheets stacked on top of each other. You draw each type of content on its own sheet. This lets you control visibility, color, and linetype for everything of one type at once. For Assignment 2 (floor plan), you might want extra layers for different electrical circuits.

#Layer NameColorLinetypeWeightWhat to draw on it
1ObjectsWhiteContinuous0.50mmAll visible outlines — walls, part edges, anything you can see
2Centre LinesYellowCENTER0.25mmCentre marks through holes, cylinders, and symmetrical features
3Hidden LinesRedDASHED0.25mmEdges hidden behind material that you can't directly see
4Construction LinesGreenContinuous0.18mmAlignment helpers and projection lines — freeze before printing
5DimensionsCyanContinuous0.18mmAll dimension lines, extension lines, numbers, labels, room text
6Title BlockOrangeContinuous0.50mmThe sheet border, title box, your name and registration number
💡
For Assignment 2 (electrical floor plan) you can add extra layers for wiring: e.g. "Circuit_Blue", "Circuit_Red", "Circuit_Green", each with a different colour and dashed linetype. This makes it easy to show/hide each circuit. The assignment says "coloured lines" so multiple wiring layers is a good approach.

Step-by-Step: Create Layers in AutoCAD

LA ← Type this and press Enter to open Layer Properties Manager
→ Click the star icon (New Layer) — do this 6 times
→ Name each layer by double-clicking the name
→ Click the COLOR square to change color
→ Click CONTINUOUS in the Linetype column to change linetype
If DASHED / CENTER not listed: click Load → find them → OK
→ Click the lineweight column to set 0.50 or 0.25mm
→ Close the dialog when done

To switch layers while drawing:
→ Look at the top-left ribbon — there's a layer dropdown box
→ Click it and select the layer you want before drawing

AutoCAD — Full Beginner Setup

You just installed it. Step-by-step from zero.

🖥️
The AutoCAD interface has 4 key parts:
1. Drawing area (the big black space in the middle — this is your paper)
2. Command line (the bar at the bottom — TYPE all commands here, watch it for instructions)
3. Ribbon toolbar (tabs at the top — most things are also clickable here)
4. Layer dropdown (top left — shows your current layer, click to switch)

The command line is the most important thing. Always watch it — it tells you what to do next. If you're stuck, press Esc twice to clear and start fresh.

UNITS ← type, Enter

Turn On Essential Modes

  • F7 = Grid (optional visual reference)

These toggle on/off. You'll see indicators in the status bar at the bottom.

3

Create All 6 Layers (see Section 07)

LA ← Layer Manager → create 6 layers with correct settings
4

Draw the Sheet Border

Switch to Title Block layer. Draw an A3 sheet:

REC ← Rectangle
→ Click anywhere · type: @420,297 · Enter
Then draw inner margin (10mm inside):
REC → click 10mm inside first corner
→ type: @400,277 · Enter
5

Draw Title Block Box

In the bottom-right corner of your sheet, draw a rectangle ~130mm wide × 50mm tall for the title block. Add text inside:

MTEXT ← Multi-line text
→ Click and drag to create text box

Plan Your Layout with Construction Lines

Switch to Construction Lines layer. Use XLINE to mark out where each view goes before drawing anything.

AutoCAD Commands — Complete Reference

Every command you need for both assignments

📝 Draw Commands

  • L — Line (click or type coordinates)
  • REC — Rectangle (@width,height)
  • C — Circle (click centre, type radius)
  • ARC — Arc (curved line / door swing)
  • XL — Construction line (infinite)
  • PL — Polyline (connected lines, one object)
  • SPL — Spline (smooth curve for wiring)
  • EL — Ellipse
  • H — Hatch (fill a closed area with pattern)
  • DONUT — Filled circle / ring

✏️ Modify Commands

  • TR — Trim (cut lines at intersection)
  • EX — Extend (stretch line to meet another)
  • E — Erase / Delete
  • M — Move (pick up and place)
  • CO — Copy
  • RO — Rotate
  • SC — Scale (resize)
  • MI — Mirror (flip)
  • O — Offset (parallel copy at a distance)
  • F — Fillet (round corner, or join two lines)
  • CHA — Chamfer (angled corner)
  • EXPLODE — Break a group into parts
  • JOIN — Join separate lines into one

📏 Dimension Commands

  • DLI — Linear (horizontal or vertical)
  • DAL — Aligned (angled dimension)
  • DDI — Diameter (for circles)
  • DRA — Radius (for arcs)
  • DAN — Angular (for angles)
  • QDIM — Quick dimension
  • DIMSTYLE — Edit dimension appearance
  • MTEXT — Multi-line text label
  • TEXT — Single-line text
  • DDEDIT — Edit existing text

🔍 View & Navigate

  • Z → E — Zoom extents (fit all)
  • Z → A — Zoom all
  • Scroll wheel — Zoom in/out
  • Middle-click drag — Pan
  • REGEN — Refresh (fix display glitches)
  • ZOOM — Zoom to window

⌨️ Must-Know Shortcuts

  • Esc — Cancel current command
  • Enter / Space — Confirm / repeat last
  • Ctrl+Z — Undo
  • Ctrl+Y — Redo
  • Ctrl+S — Save
  • F8 — ORTHO on/off
  • F3 — OSNAP on/off
  • F7 — Grid on/off
  • Delete — Delete selected

🗂️ Layer & Properties

  • LA — Layer Manager
  • LTSCALE — Adjust dash spacing (try 0.5 if dashes look solid)
  • LWEIGHT — Toggle lineweight display
  • PROPERTIES — Edit any object's properties
  • MA — Match properties (copy style from one object to another)
⚡
How to type coordinates in AutoCAD:
@100,50 = 100mm right, 50mm up from last point (relative)
@100<30 = 100mm at 30 degrees from last point (polar)
100,50 (no @) = absolute position from origin (0,0) of drawing
Always use @ (relative) for drawing — you usually don't care about absolute position.

Common Beginner Problems & Fixes

❓

My dashed lines look solid

Type LTSCALE → Enter → type 0.5 → Enter. If still solid, try 0.3 or 0.25. The scale depends on how zoomed in you are.

❓

I can't see my drawing — it disappeared

Type Z then E (Zoom Extents). This zooms to fit everything on screen. Or type REGEN then Z A.

❓

Lines won't snap to each other

Press F3 to turn OSNAP on. Or right-click the OSNAP button in the status bar and enable Endpoint, Midpoint, and Center snaps.

❓

My lines are going diagonal instead of straight

Press F8 to turn ORTHO on. This forces all lines to be perfectly horizontal or vertical.

❓

I accidentally drew on the wrong layer

Select the objects → look in the layer dropdown at top left → change it to the correct layer. The objects will move to the right layer.

Frequently Asked Questions

❓ My lines are too thin / too thick when I print

In AutoCAD, set lineweights correctly in the Layer Manager (0.5mm for visible, 0.25mm for hidden/centre). When plotting, make sure "Plot with plot styles" or "Use lineweights" is checked in the Plot dialog. If printing to PDF, use the DWG to PDF.pc3 plotter and check lineweight settings.

❓ How do I make curved wiring lines?

Use SPL (Spline command). Click your start point, click several intermediate points to guide the curve, then press Enter twice to finish. You can also use ARC for simple single curves. Make sure you're on the correct wiring layer so the colour and linetype apply automatically.

❓ What scale should I draw the floor plan at?

Draw everything in AutoCAD at real size in mm (e.g., a 5m wall = 5000mm). When you go to print/plot, set the scale to 1:50 or 1:100 depending on how big the house is. Write the chosen scale in your title block.

❓ How do I add the hatch/shading to walls?

The walls must be closed shapes for hatch to work. Type H → in the hatch dialog, choose a pattern (try ANSI31 for diagonal lines, or AR-CONC for a concrete look). Click "Add: Pick Points" then click inside the wall boundary. If it doesn't work, the boundary might not be fully closed — use F (Fillet) to ensure corners are exactly joined.

❓ How do I draw the electrical symbols without a block library?

Draw them manually using basic shapes: circles for lights, rectangles for sockets, arcs for fans. After drawing one symbol, use CO (Copy) to place multiple copies around the drawing. Or create a Block: draw the symbol, type B (Block), select all parts, name it (e.g. "LIGHT"), pick an insertion point. Then use I (Insert) to place it anywhere.

❓ What folder name format for submission?

The assignment says use your registration number as the folder name, like: ENG-219-001-2025. Put both .DWG files (assignment 1 and assignment 2) inside the same folder. Hand the folder (on USB or however your class does it) to the class rep before Tuesday 21/04/2026.
